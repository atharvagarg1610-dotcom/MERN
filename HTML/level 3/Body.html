SWAMI VIVEKANANDA

SWAMI VIVEKANANDA (1863-1902) was an Indian Hindu monk and a key figure in the introduction of Indian philosophies of Vedanta and Yoga to the Western world. He is best known for his inspiring speech at the Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago in 1893, where he began with the words "Sisters and brothers of America." Vivekananda's teachings emphasized the unity of all religions, the importance of self-realization, and the potential for human beings to achieve greatness through spiritual practice. He founded the Ramakrishna Mission, which continues to promote his ideals and engage in social service activities worldwide. Swami Vivekananda's legacy continues to inspire people around the world, and his teachings are celebrated annually on National Youth Day in India, which falls on his birthday, January 12th. swami vivekananda was an influential spiritual leader and philosopher who played a significant role in the revival of Hinduism in India and the spread of Indian spirituality in the West. He was born in Kolkata (then Calcutta) and was a disciple of the mystic Ramakrishna Paramahamsa. Vivekananda's teachings emphasized the importance of self-realization, the unity of all religions, and the potential for human beings to achieve greatness through spiritual practice. He is remembered for his powerful speeches and writings, which continue to inspire people around the world to this day.
